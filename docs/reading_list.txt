TIER 1 — MUST READ BEFORE ERICA CALL (6 papers)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Direct competitors / foundational framing. Skip these and you'll either
re-derive their findings or look like you missed the field.

[T1] Rationality Check! Benchmarking the Rationality of Large Language Models
     arxiv 2509.14546  (Sept 2025)
     → Most direct competitor for the rationality-benchmark framing. Frames
       rationality as foundational AGI infrastructure. Use their "theoretical
       vs practical rationality" vocabulary; it maps onto our ordinal vs
       cardinal split.

[T1] Benchmarking the Rationality of AI Decision Making via Transitivity  [AXIS-2]
     arxiv 2502.10554  (Feb 2025)
     → Bayesian model selection on transitivity for 10 Llama 2/3 variants.
       Key finding: base models pass; chat/instruct variants violate.
       This is the empirical anchor for "instruction-tuning hurts rationality"
       — the pattern our Sonnet/Haiku results fit into.

[T1] Alignment Revisited: Are Large Language Models Consistent in Stated and
     Revealed Preferences?
     arxiv 2506.00751  (June 2025)
     → Direct framing match for our work. Across Claude/GPT/Llama families,
       LLMs systematically contradict stated preferences when faced with
       actual choices. Predicts a gap will exist in our setting too.

[T1] LLM Economicus? Mapping the Behavioral Biases of LLMs via Utility Theory  [AXIS-3]
     arxiv 2408.02784  (Aug 2024)
     → Methodologically closest published precedent. Uses utility theory
       directly as evaluation framework, same lens as ours. Findings:
       GPT-4 shows loss aversion ≈ humans; "most LLMs struggle to maintain
       consistent economic behavior across settings" (= our Sonnet/Haiku result).

[T1] GuruAgents: Emulating Wise Investors with Prompt-Guided LLM Agents
     arxiv 2510.01664  (Oct 2025)
     → CLOSEST PARALLEL WORK that we should not be ignorant of. They built
       a Warren-Buffett-prompted LLM agent + 4 others, evaluated by backtest
       (42.2% CAGR on NASDAQ-100 Q4 2023 – Q2 2025). Our work is complementary:
       they measure returns; we measure revealed-preference fit.

[T1] PTCBENCH: Benchmarking Contextual Stability of Personality Traits in
     LLM Systems
     arxiv 2602.00016
     → 39,240 personality records × 12 situational contexts. Key finding:
       "Unemployment" scenario triggers significant personality changes.
       Sets the rigor bar for our §13.1 adversarial-rationality extension.
       Same shape as our proposed pressure-stability test, different evaluation
       primitive (personality dims vs rationality axioms).

[T1] QEDBENCH: Quantifying the Alignment Gap in Automated Evaluation of
     University-Level Mathematical Proofs
     Gonzalez et al. (Yale-led 51-author benchmark) — arxiv 2602.20629
     (Feb 2026)
     → Direct empirical anchor for our §1.5.7 judge-free-by-design claim and
       §22.8 forcing function. Measures LLM-as-judge alignment gap on math
       proofs (verifiable ground-truth domain) and finds +0.18 to +0.36 mean
       score inflation across 4 of 7 frontier judge models (Claude Opus 4.5,
       DeepSeek-V3, Qwen 2.5 Max, Llama 4 Maverick). Methodologically novel:
       7-judge × 5-solver matrix over 1000+ proofs with 1000+ hours of expert
       human evaluation as ground truth + dual-rubric design (course-specific
       vs expert-common-knowledge). Adopt: the N×M matrix evaluation pattern
       (for us: measurement models × LLMs under test, with held-out human
       reference set as sanity check). Reject: their "closer-to-human = better"
       normative framing, which §1.5.1 explicitly rejects. Their finding that
       reasoning-specialized models degrade in specific domains (discrete math,
       graph theory) is also worth replicating: do reasoning-specialized models
       show analogous axiom-satisfaction degradation in specific decision
       domains? Could become a AgentEcon Readiness headline finding. Yale-QEDBench
       GitHub repo open.

[T1] TERMS-BENCH: Diagnosing LLM Negotiation Agents Beyond Deal Rate  [AXIS-1]
     Zhang, Zhang, Pappu, El, Blanchet, Athey, Liu, Zou — arxiv 2605.13909
     (May 2026, Stanford School of Engineering + Dept of Economics + GSB)
     PDF copy: docs/papers/10_TermsBench_2605.13909.pdf
     → Bayesian-game framework for bilateral price negotiation with
       environment-as-verifier (direct §1.5.7 external validation; quoted in
       §1.5.7 plan). 4-axis 6-metric design: surplus efficiency (SE+),
       agreement calibration (AGR+/CSE+/FAGR-), opponent modeling (BEtype),
       protocol compliance (CritViol%). Six counterpart families (CANDID /
       TACITURN / EXPRESSIVE / STRATEGIC + STOCHASTIC / ADVERSARIAL stress)
       for capability isolation. Three regimes (Overlap / Urgency-shift /
       No-deal). Oracle is *simulator-relative* Bayes-optimal — explicitly
       NOT normative. Key methodological contribution we adopt in §15.6:
       intervention-based oracle-gap decomposition (Δ_inf + Δ_unc + Δ_ctrl
       via base / oracle-posterior / revealed-type interventions) substitutes
       for choice-rationality pre-checks. 13 LLM agents evaluated; Claude
       Opus 4.6 leads SE+=0.694, GLM-5.1 leads BEtype=0.218 (lowest belief
       error), GPT-4o-mini bottom at 0.189. Finding 2: αcue < 0 for every
       LLM (cue-revealing prompts REDUCE surplus extraction — agents are
       cue-fooled). Data-grounded extension on Amazon catalog (831 products,
       14 categories). Bankroll mode chaining T sessions surfaces solvency
       failures invisible to single-episode metrics. Senior advising:
       Susan Athey + Jose Blanchet + James Zou; Alvin Roth thanked for
       early feedback citing Axiomatic Models of Bargaining. Direct
       coordination target — Erica Zhang (Stanford School of Engineering)
       + James Zou Lab. v2 D2 implementation path: wrap, don't rebuild.
       Repo: github.com/zou-group/terms-bench. Leaderboard:
       terms-bench.github.io.

[T1] Revealed Rationality: Label-Free Regularization from Representation
     Theorems  [AXIS-2]
     Isaiah Andrews (MIT Dept of Economics + NBER, sole author) — Feb 2026
     PDF copy: docs/papers/revealed_rationality_mit_2026-02.pdf
     Published: economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2026-02/Revealed%20Rationality_1.pdf
     → THE academic foundation for our Tier-4 commercial path (§17,
       training-signal API). Proves that classical decision-theory
       representation theorems can be turned into LABEL-FREE, polynomial-
       time-computable continuous penalties for LLM training. Three
       instantiations: (1) de Finetti probabilistic coherence via Dutch-
       book LP — L(p) = max_b min_ωj Π(ωj), zero iff probabilities are
       coherent; (2) Afriat preference rationality via CCEI (Critical
       Cost Efficiency Index) — penalty 1 − CCEI ∈ [0,1], zero iff data
       satisfy GARP; (3) Echenique-Saito SARSEU joint beliefs+preferences
       via LP feasibility. The "if and only if" structure of representation
       theorems lets you penalize axiom violations without specifying what
       the model should value. Key citations *within Andrews* directly
       feeding our plan: Tak et al. 2026 — LLMs violate vNM but reasoning
       tokens improve compliance (anchors §1.5.6 reasoning-helps thesis);
       Chadwick et al. 2025 — explicit money-pump + Dutch-book construction
       against incoherent LLMs (commercial-pitch anchor for Tier 2/3);
       Chen et al. 2023 — GPT-3.5 CCEI > 0.997 on budget allocation
       (CEILING-EFFECT WARNING for our A1-A3 axiom dimensions: GARP may
       not differentiate at v1 frontier-model level — extend dimensions /
       more observations needed per Andrews's own note); Wen 2025 — prompt-
       based role specialization (e.g. "biotechnology expert" persona)
       SUBSTANTIALLY REDUCES GARP compliance (smoking-gun empirical
       support for §1.5.5 context-conditional axiomatization); Mazeika et
       al. 2025 — larger models satisfy EU axioms but with misaligned
       goals (validates Andrews's "coherence ≠ good" caveat which we adopt
       in §1.5.1 descriptive-vs-normative framing); Qiu et al. 2026 —
       Bayes-rule failures improvable via supervised FT on Bayesian model
       predictions. Andrews's caveat (verbatim): "coherence is not
       sufficient for good behavior... so other training signals remain
       essential." This is our exact §1.5.1 framing. Citation density
       to MIT/NBER + sole-author + early-2026 means this paper will be
       widely circulated in the LLM-economics community — cite extensively
       in our methodology paper and especially in any Tier 4 commercial
       pitch.

[T1] EconEvals: Benchmarks and Litmus Tests for Economic Decision-Making
     by LLM Agents  [AXIS-3 partial; reliability score adjacent to AXIS-2]
     Fish, Shephard, Li, Shorrer, Gonczarowski — arxiv 2503.18825
     (Feb 2026, Harvard + Penn State)
     PDF copy: docs/papers/econevals_2503.18825.pdf
     Repo: github.com/sara-fish/econ-evals-paper (open source)
     → Closest direct competitor at the Cat B-C parameter+outcome layer.
       Three economic environments (procurement / scheduling / pricing)
       via tool-use API; agent learns environment in context across 100
       periods at 3 difficulty levels (Basic / Medium / Hard). Three
       litmus tests for tradeoff revelation: Patience vs Impatience
       (intertemporal — direct overlap with our B2), Efficiency vs Equality
       (task allocation), Collusiveness vs Competitiveness (multi-agent
       pricing). Each litmus emits three scores: LITMUS (tradeoff response),
       RELIABILITY (coherence of choice behavior — test-retest, NOT GARP),
       COMPETENCY (capability under single objective — operationalized as a
       PRE-CHECK following Ross et al. 2024 / Fish et al. 2024; if
       competency fails, litmus score is uninterpretable). Direct §1.5.7
       validation quote: "These evaluations measure LLM tradeoff responses
       using human labels—which can be resource-intensive—or LLM-as-a-judge
       labels—which can be unreliable. By contrast, our litmus tests
       utilize stylized environments, which means the metrics that measure
       tradeoff responses are directly grounded in an underlying
       theoretical model." Models tested: Claude-3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5
       Pro, GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, o4-mini, Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-5, Gemini 3 Pro
       Preview. Key findings: capability improving over time
       (procurement +41 pp/yr, scheduling +50 pp/yr, pricing +18 pp/yr);
       reasoning-model o4-mini scores 3rd-best in procurement (calculation-
       heavy) but 5th-6th elsewhere; high reliability + competency across
       frontier models. v2 implication: if we expand beyond investment
       into procurement / scheduling / pricing, consider wrapping
       EconEvals rather than rebuilding — same pattern as TERMS-Bench
       for negotiation. Distinguishes from us by NOT testing axiom layer
       (Cat A); EconEvals's reliability is measurement-test-retest, not
       choice-theoretic GARP.

[T1] The Emergence of Economic Rationality of GPT  [AXIS-2; §7.A1 ceiling + §1.5.5 framing-sensitivity]
     Chen, Liu, Shan, Zhong — PNAS 2023, arxiv 2305.12763
     (Lingnan + Tsinghua + HKUST + NUS)
     PDF copy: docs/papers/cited/llm_rationality_core/chen_liu_shan_zhong_2023_emergence_economic_rationality_GPT_arxiv2305.12763.pdf
     → GPT-3.5-Turbo tested on 25-task budget allocation × 4 domains
       (risk, time, social, food) × 100 repetitions = 10,000 tasks.
       Rationality measured via GARP compliance. GPT outperforms 347-
       person human sample (US representative). CCEI > 0.997 mean across
       domains — the source of our §7.A1 ceiling-effect warning. KEY
       NUANCE often missed: rationality is robust to demographic settings
       (age, gender) but BREAKS SIGNIFICANTLY under different price
       framing OR discrete choice setting — direct empirical anchor for
       §1.5.5 context-conditional axiomatization. GPT preference
       parameters are substantially more homogeneous than humans.
       Dual-purpose anchor: ceiling-effect warning AND framing-sensitivity
       evidence. Cite both ways in methodology paper §2 (related work).

[T1] Thinking Fast and Slow in Large Language Models  [AXIS-2; §1.5.6 scaling narrative]
     Hagendorff, Fabi, Kosinski — arxiv 2212.05206
     (published Nature Computational Science 2023, 10.1038/s43588-023-00527-x;
     Stuttgart + UCSD + Stanford)
     PDF copy: docs/papers/cited/llm_rationality_core/hagendorff_fabi_kosinski_2023_thinking_fast_slow_arxiv2212.05206.pdf
     → Tested CRT (Cognitive Reflection Test) + semantic illusions on
       GPT-1 → ChatGPT-4 family. 50 bespoke task versions to prevent
       training-data contamination (200 tasks total). 500 human Prolific
       participants (38% CRT correct vs 41% original literature). Two
       findings: (1) GPT-1 → GPT-3 scaling → MORE human-like intuitive
       errors emerge (System 1 thinking with scale); (2) ChatGPT-3.5/4
       transition → models avoid the traps EVEN WHEN PREVENTED FROM
       CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT REASONING (System-1-like next-word generation
       becomes more accurate without explicit System-2 deliberation).
       Precursor to Tak et al. 2026's reasoning-helps story. Together
       Hagendorff + Tak tell the 4-stage scaling narrative for §1.5.6
       measurement-model pluralism: scaling makes worse → ChatGPT fixes
       → reasoning helps → emotion steering reverses. Cite as the
       "context matters" empirical foundation.

[T1] Bayesian Teaching Enables Probabilistic Reasoning in LLMs  [AXIS-5; §17 Tier 4 third academic foundation]
     Qiu, Sha, Allen, Kim, Linzen, van Steenkiste — arxiv 2503.17523
     (Nature Communications 2026, 10.1038/s41467-025-67998-6;
     MIT + Meta + DeepMind + UBC + Vector + Google Research + NYU)
     PDF copy: docs/papers/cited/llm_rationality_core/qiu_2026_bayesian_teaching_probabilistic_reasoning_arxiv2503.17523.pdf
     → LLMs tested on multi-round flight-recommendation requiring
       Bayesian belief updating. 5 LLMs (Gemma 2 9B/27B, Llama 3 8B/70B,
       Qwen 2.5 7B/32B, GPT-4.1 Mini, Gemini 1.5 Pro) + Bayesian
       Assistant normative oracle. LLMs plateau after 1 round of feedback
       (~37-40% accuracy); Bayesian Assistant reaches 81% after 5 rounds;
       humans 39% → 47% (slight improvement but well below normative).
       KEY CONTRIBUTION: "Bayesian teaching" — fine-tune LLM on Bayesian
       Assistant TRACES (showing reasoning under uncertainty), not on
       correct-answer traces. Bayesian model is better teacher than
       oracle because it shows reasoning, not just answers. Method
       generalizes to NEW tasks (hotel + web shopping). Relevance to
       AERead: THIRD academic foundation for §17 Tier 4 alongside
       Andrews 2026 (training-time penalty) and Chadwick 2025 (inference-
       time remediation). Three complementary mechanisms become three
       Tier 4 product variants. Also Axis 5 metacog remediation precedent:
       belief-updating failures fix via supervised mimicry of Bayesian
       traces.

[T1] Dutch Books and Money Pumps: Rectifying Vulnerabilities in LLMs Through Rationality  [AXIS-2 + remediation; §17 Tier 4 second academic foundation]
     Chadwick, Kahng, Kipper — HAR 2025 workshop, Paris
     (University of Rochester)
     PDF copy: docs/papers/cited/llm_rationality_core/chadwick_kahng_kipper_2025_dutch_books_money_pumps.pdf
     URL: https://ansonkahng.com/docs/papers/llmrationality.pdf
     → CRITICAL update from my prior framing: this paper does MORE than
       Dutch-book / money-pump exploitation — it proposes a CONSTRUCTIVE
       RATIONALITY LAYER that rectifies both vulnerabilities. Two
       technical contributions: (1) Probabilistic coherence via linear
       pooling over N=100 sampled distributions + quadratic-programming
       to minimally distort probabilities to satisfy axioms; (2)
       Preference transitivity via Iterative Max Directed Cut (IMDC) —
       hierarchical voting rule approximating Kemeny ranking in
       polynomial time. Three IMDC variants (x-IMDC-o, x-IMDC-u, x ∈
       {Kemeny, Copeland, Borda, Plurality, Veto}) with theorems
       comparing acyclic vs cyclic tournament graphs. Explicitly rebuts
       Goldstein's "LLMs cannot in principle be coherent" architectural
       argument. Relevance: SECOND academic foundation for §17 Tier 4
       (inference-time remediation layer); paired with Andrews 2026
       (training-time penalty) and Qiu 2026 (supervised fine-tuning on
       normative traces) for the 3-mechanism product framing. Also our
       diagnostic page §6 "Counterfactual scenarios" can use Chadwick
       IMDC as the concrete "if transitivity were fixed" implementation.

[T2] Insights from the Inverse: Reconstructing LLM Training Goals Through
     Inverse Reinforcement Learning
     arxiv 2410.12491  (Oct 2024)
     → IRL on RLHF-trained LLMs. 85% accuracy recovering training reward
       function (toxicity domain). Validates IRL methodology on LLM
       outputs. Their non-identifiability handling = analog of our parameter
       stability story.

[T2] Inverse Reinforcement Learning Meets Large Language Model Post-Training:
     Basics, Advances, and Opportunities
     arxiv 2507.13158  (July 2025)
     → Survey paper. Foundational reading for situating our work in the
       broader IRL/LLM landscape. Read selectively (it's long).

[T2] TRAIT: Do LLMs Have Distinct and Consistent Personality? A Personality
     Testset Designed for LLMs with Psychometrics
     ACL Anthology / NAACL Findings 2025
     → Psychometrically-validated personality testset. Highest scores across
       content validity, internal validity, refusal rate, reliability.
       Methodologically rigorous. Useful pattern for designing our axiom tests.

[T2] The Moral Mind(s) of Large Language Models
     arxiv 2412.04476  (Dec 2024)
     → Tested ~40 models across providers on ethical scenarios. "At least
       one model per provider exhibits behavior consistent with approximately
       stable moral principles, effectively acting as if nearly optimizing
       a utility function." Direct empirical support for our framing.

[T2] Can an LLM Learn Preferences from Choice Data?
     arxiv 2401.07345  (2024)
     → Structurally IDENTICAL setup to ours. Simulate-Recommend-Evaluate
       framework tests LLM preference recovery from synthetic choice data
       (disappointment-aversion theory). Different application (their theory
       vs our portfolio utility), same methodological shape.

[T2] Do Large Language Models Learn Human-Like Strategic Preferences?
     arxiv 2404.08710  (2024)
     → Tests whether open-source LLMs exhibit stable human-like strategic
       preferences using game theory as a comparison. Foundational for
       the "is LLM rationality stable across contexts?" question.

== Context-conditional axiomatization (for v2 dimension D1 + paper #2) ==

Foundational reading for the §1.5.5 product corollary: "agents should be
context-conditional about which axiomatization to honor, not globally
optimized for one." No single paper covers this for LLMs (the gap is the
research opportunity). The entries below establish the building blocks.

-- Decision-theory foundations (multiple coherent axiomatizations exist) --

[T3] Knightian Decision Theory, Part I
     Truman F. Bewley (2002) — Decisions in Economics and Finance 25
     → Foundational paper for relaxing the COMPLETENESS axiom. Agents may
       reasonably refuse to rank some pairs. Used in §1.5.3 table; cite when
       the methodology paper discusses non-vNM axiomatizations.

[T3] Maxmin Expected Utility with Non-Unique Prior
     Gilboa, Schmeidler (1989) — Journal of Mathematical Economics 18
     → Foundational paper for ambiguity-averse decision-making. Drops
       INDEPENDENCE in a specific way. Relevant when the agent's context is
       Ellsberg-like uncertainty (genuine ambiguity, not just risk).

[T3] Regret Theory: An Alternative Theory of Rational Choice Under Uncertainty
     Loomes, Sugden (1982) — Economic Journal 92
     → Foundational paper for TRANSITIVITY-violating but coherent preferences.
       Cycling can be rational if outcomes are evaluated relative to
       counterfactual regret. Key reference for §1.5.5 risk discussion: a
       model violating transitivity may be reasoning regret-theoretically.

[T3] Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk
     Kahneman, Tversky (1979) — Econometrica 47
     → The empirically-grounded relaxation of INDEPENDENCE that anchors
       Category B of our benchmark. Already implicitly cited via §7.B1; add
       to reading list explicitly as the foundational reference.

[T3] A Theory of Anticipated Utility
     Quiggin (1982) — Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization 3
     → Rank-dependent utility theory; alternative to KT prospect theory for
       relaxing independence. Useful when comparing axiomatization families.

-- Empirical LLM context-sensitivity (the substrate this would build on) --

[T3] PTCBENCH (already T1 above)
     → 12 situational contexts modulate personality-trait expression in LLMs.
       Methodologically the closest empirical pattern to "do LLMs adapt
       axiomatic behavior to context?" The v2 D1 dimension is structurally
       PTCBench-for-axioms.

[T3] Alignment Revisited (already T1 above)
     → Stated-vs-revealed gap is itself a form of context-conditional
       behavior. If the model behaves differently when "framed as stating
       preferences" vs "framed as making choices," that is already evidence
       of context-conditional axiomatization (just unintentional).

-- Mechanisms for axiom-set switching --

[T3] Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback
     Bai et al. (Anthropic, 2022) — arxiv 2212.08073
     → Constitutional AI / RLAIF as the training-level mechanism for
       conditioning model behavior on declared principles. Closest existing
       template for "training-level axiomatization equipment" (Tier 4 in our
       §17, training-level row in the §1.5.5 table).

[T3] Switch Transformer / Mixtral / generic MoE survey
     Fedus, Zoph, Shazeer (2021) — arxiv 2101.03961 (Switch Transformer)
     Jiang et al. (2024) — arxiv 2401.04088 (Mixtral)
     → Architectural template for the "router / MoE" row in §1.5.5: meta-
       agent dispatching to axiomatization-specialized sub-agents. Both
       references useful when the v2 paper discusses architectural options.

[T3] Tree of Thoughts / Self-Consistency reasoning scaffolds
     Yao et al. (2023) — arxiv 2305.10601 (ToT)
     Wang et al. (2022) — arxiv 2203.11171 (Self-Consistency)
     → Inference-time scaffolding row in the §1.5.5 table. Demonstrates the
       cheaper end of the cost/reliability spectrum: enforce axiomatic
       reasoning via CoT structure rather than training.

-- Adversarial robustness (the §1.5.5 manipulation-vector risk) --

[T3] Many-shot Jailbreaking
     Anil et al. (Anthropic, 2024)
     → Long-context jailbreaks bypass safety alignment. Same mechanism would
       apply to bypassing a declared axiomatization. The §1.5.5 risk
       ("selector is a manipulation vector") needs this reference for the v2
       paper's threat model.

[T3] Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned LLMs
     Zou et al. (2023) — arxiv 2307.15043
     → Adversarial-suffix attacks. Together with many-shot jailbreaking,
       establishes that prompt-level axiomatization declarations
       (§1.5.5 row 1) are brittle even before considering deliberate attacks.
